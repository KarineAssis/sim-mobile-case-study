# SIM Mobile — Data Analytics and Digital Transformation Case Study

## Executive summary

SIM Mobile was a digital transformation initiative in industrial maintenance that illustrates a core Analytics principle: **the quality of insights depends on the quality and timing of data capture**.

The main issue was the distance between work execution and data entry. Technicians working on the production floor still had to walk to fixed computers to check inventory, create or update maintenance work orders, record labor time, and close services. When data entry was postponed, the system no longer reflected operational reality at the right time.

I led the initiative from the business side, translating the operational problem into functional requirements, testing, pilot activities, rollout and measurement opportunities.

## Context

Before the solution, part of the maintenance administrative routine depended on computers located in support rooms. When immediate access was not available, records could be entered later.

This process created:

- movement between the production area and support rooms;
- waiting for an available computer;
- data entry after the work had been completed;
- work orders remaining open for longer;
- labor records entered later;
- lower alignment between system data and operational reality;
- a less reliable basis for indicators and analysis.

### Data problem chain

`field execution → movement and waiting → delayed data entry → open or incomplete work orders → lower data quality → limited insights`

## Objective

Make the most relevant maintenance workflows available on mobile devices and bring data entry closer to the point and time of work.

## Solution

SIM Mobile was developed as an extension of the most relevant corporate maintenance-system workflows, rather than a full replica of the desktop version.

The available functions included:

- corrective maintenance;
- creation and update of maintenance work orders;
- inventory consultation;
- preventive maintenance;
- labor allocation consultation;
- material requisition.

## Pilot and rollout

The pilot started with electrical maintenance teams and a controlled functional scope.

The validation cycle was:

`development → testing → field use → issue reporting → correction → retesting`

The rollout involved:

1. user authorization;
2. training preparation;
3. corporate Wi-Fi access request;
4. system access enablement;
5. start of mobile usage.

The PCM team was trained first so it could support the other maintenance teams during expansion.

## Documented impact

The available evidence supports that the project:

- moved system activities closer to the point of work;
- allowed records to be entered closer to the time of execution;
- reduced dependency on computers located in support rooms;
- connected execution and data entry more directly;
- created a better basis for updated operational data;
- strengthened collaboration between maintenance and technology.

The available data does not support attributing isolated financial gains, exact time reduction, or quantified productivity improvement to the project.

## Analytics opportunity

A next phase could integrate access logs, work-order events and performance indicators to evaluate adoption, efficiency and operational impact.

### Adoption

- monthly active users;
- conversion from enabled access to actual usage;
- usage frequency;
- adoption by feature, team, and unit.

### Operational efficiency

- work-order closure time;
- same-shift closure rate;
- time between execution and data entry;
- mobile versus desktop share.

### Data quality

- work orders without labor allocation;
- aging of open work orders;
- late-entry rate;
- incomplete work-order rate.

### Business performance

- technician productive time;
- maintenance backlog;
- equipment availability;
- correlation between solution usage and MTTR.
